# Round 2 — model ladder on the 2026 slice

**Run:** 2026-06-18 · `claude-haiku-4.5` and `claude-sonnet-4.6` (all three arms),
`claude-opus-4.8` (memory arm only) · 134 items (2026-f) · n=1. 2026-f is the freshest
paper and the most discriminating slice (see [haiku pilot](./2026-06_haiku-pilot.md),
Reading 4). **±9% band at n=134** — read the pattern, not single cells. Haiku also has an n=3
reliability top-up (see Reliability, below); opus web/patrick weren't run (cost).

**Arms:** `none` (memory) · `web` (general web search) · `patrick` (verbatim EPC
retrieval). **Two axes, weighted equally:** an attorney needs the **correct verdict**
*and* the **correct law cited** — a right answer with no/wrong citation isn't
verifiable, and a citation without the right answer is worse. Both are reported in full.

**Metrics:** *answer* = verdict correct · *citation recall* = of the required
provision(s), the fraction cited · *citation precision* = of the provisions cited, the
fraction that were required (unresolvable cites count against it) · *fully-correct* =
verdict right **and** citation recall 100% **and** precision 100% (right answer, all
required law cited, nothing spurious — the strict joint metric) · *hallucination* =
share of cited provisions that don't resolve · *retrieval-recall* (patrick only) = of
the required provision(s), the fraction the tools surfaced, whether or not cited.

## Verdict axis

Grounding lifts the verdict most on the weak model and converges at the top. haiku:
none 69 → web 85 → patrick 93. sonnet: 88 / 91 / 92, within the band. opus from memory
alone is 93. So answer accuracy **saturates as the model strengthens** — a strong base
model already gets these single-provision verdicts right, and web ≈ patrick at the top.
Bigger models are better here in absolute terms (none 69 → 88 → 93): **strength is
worth paying for, and grounding adds on top, largest where the model is weakest.**

## Citation axis (equally weighted)

This is where the base model is weakest, and it does **not** close with scale the way
the verdict does:

- **Base models answer better than they cite.** haiku-none 69% verdict / 32% citation;
  sonnet-none 88% / 72%; opus-none 93% / 82%. A large share of correct verdicts arrive
  without the governing provision — not usable to an attorney as-is.
- **Both grounded arms raise citation sharply, most on the weak model.** Citation
  recall: haiku 32 → web 63 → patrick 79; sonnet 72 → 81 → 82. Patrick leads on
  citation **precision** at haiku (63 vs web 52 vs none 26) and is level at sonnet
  (57–59 across arms).
- **Citation precision is the weakest metric for everyone** (26–63%): over-citation is
  common, and even grounded it tops out ~63%. Because `fully-correct` requires
  precision = 100%, this is what most caps it.
- **Hallucination is low for the grounded arms** (4–6%) and worst for the weak base
  model (haiku-none 11%).

## Both together: fully-correct

The joint metric — right verdict *and* the required law cited cleanly — is what an
attorney actually receives:

- haiku: none **16** → web 40 → patrick **50** (the highest fully-correct cell in the
  grid).
- sonnet: none 41 / web 38 / patrick 42 — a three-way tie inside the band.
- opus-none: 37.

So on the weaker model, grounding roughly **triples** the rate of complete, cited-
correct answers (16 → 50); on the stronger models all three arms land ~40, because a
strong base model is already decent on both axes and the single-provision items leave
little headroom. The honest reading is that the *large, clear* joint-correctness win is
on the weaker model; on strong models the arms converge within noise on this dataset.

## Why each arm fails

Of the wrong answers, the *kind* of failure differs sharply (patrick-arm "had-the-law"
= retrieved and cited the gold, still reasoned wrong):

| | wrong | retrieval miss | had-the-law, reasoned wrong |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| haiku none | 41 | 41 | 0 |
| haiku patrick | 9 | 4 | 4 |
| sonnet none | 16 | 16 | 0 |
| sonnet patrick | 11 | 3 | 6 |

Base models fail because they can't get the law (100% retrieval miss). Patrick fails
mostly because it had the law and reasoned wrong — fewer errors, and of a kind a
stronger model reduces rather than a grounding failure.

## Retrieval mechanics (where patrick can improve)

- **Retrieval-recall is high** — 88% (haiku) and 96% (sonnet) on this slice, 94% on the
  full 418. The tools surface the governing provision the large majority of the time;
  the unsurfaced residual (4–12%) is a retrieval-engine gap (coverage + query quality).
- **The bigger gap is retrieved-but-not-cited:** retr-rec − cite-rec = 9pp (haiku),
  **14pp (sonnet)**, 5pp (full 418). The model surfaced the right law and didn't cite
  it — sonnet especially (retrieves 96%, cites 82%). The clearest, cheapest fix: cite
  what you ground on.

## Reliability (n=3, haiku)

Each haiku item was answered three times; reliability is the share of items whose
**modal verdict reproduces** across the runs (the `relia` metric):

| arm | reliability | answer accuracy (modal of 3) |
|---|--:|--:|
| none | 89% | 69% |
| web | 93% | 86% |
| patrick | 96% | 95% |

Grounding makes the verdict **more reproducible** (89 → 93 → 96), so it adds a third
benefit alongside accuracy and citation — stability, which matters when an attorney
needs the same answer twice. Modal-of-3 answer accuracy (69 / 86 / 95) tracks the
single-run figures (69 / 85 / 93), confirming those weren't a fluke.

## Scope — and why it fits prosecution

The items test one capability: recognise the governing provision, retrieve it, and
judge a statement about it — single-provision law lookup on public EP law (435/437
single-gold). That is deliberately matched to the near-term product. A prosecution
assistant's core job is to recognise, retrieve, and understand the law an examiner
cites — examiners cite the provision for a rejection, and it's largely keyword-driven —
not to reason across a fact pattern. So this benchmark validates the
**retrieval/grounding layer** prosecution depends on, and shows the engine does it well
(retrieval-recall 88–96%, strong citation, more reproducible answers). The workflow on
top of that layer — pulling the cited provision, explaining it, helping draft the
response — is product, not benchmark.

What it does **not** test — citing *all* of several provisions (completeness) and
fact-pattern application — is "everything-assistant" territory, a later product stage.
That is also why the arms converge on strong models here: single-provision public law
is near-ceiling for a frontier model, and that's fine, because the value is reliable
recognition/retrieval/grounding, not out-reasoning the model. A future round 3
(multi-provision + fact-pattern) would test that harder scope if and when the product
reaches for it.
